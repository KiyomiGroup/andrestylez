Lifemap Naija —
Mental Wellness App

Mobile App Design
Product Designer

Figma
iOS / Android (Concept)

Project Overview

Lifemap Naija is a mobile app concept designed for Nigerian users combining mental wellness support with a passion and purpose discovery experience. The product addresses a real gap: most wellness apps are designed for Western audiences and don't speak to the social, cultural, or economic realities of life in Nigeria.

The design challenge was to create an app that is emotionally resonant, accessible to users across varying levels of smartphone literacy, and culturally grounded while still feeling modern and premium.

"Wellness tools built for Lagos must understand Lagos first its pace, its pressure, its people."

Problem Statement

Young Nigerians experience significant mental health challenges economic pressure, career uncertainty, identity formation in a rapidly changing society yet the tools available are either clinically detached, culturally foreign, or both.

The specific problem Lifemap Naija was designed to solve:

  • Lack of accessible, relatable mental wellness tools designed for Nigerian users
  • No existing app combines passion discovery with emotional wellbeing in one experience
  • Existing apps have high friction onboarding discouraging casual users
  • Most wellness apps don't reflect African visual culture or communication styles

User & Business Goals

User Goals:

  • Understand and manage their emotional state with simple, daily check-ins
  • Discover their interests, strengths, and possible life paths through guided prompts
  • Feel seen and understood by an app that reflects their lived experience
  • Access wellness support without stigma or clinical intimidation

Business Goals:

  • Build a product that can grow through word-of-mouth and community resonance
  • Create a freemium experience with clear upgrade triggers
  • Establish brand credibility in the growing African wellness market

Design Process

Research Phase

I began by researching existing mental health and self-discovery apps mapping their strengths and failure points for Nigerian audiences. Key insights included that users preferred conversational interfaces over clinical questionnaires, and that visual warmth was important for emotional engagement.

Wireframe Exploration

I explored three different structural approaches for the main dashboard:

  • Card-based feed (familiar, low learning curve)
  • Mode-switching tabs (clear separation of features)
  • Integrated home screen with mood as the central anchor (chosen approach)

The third approach was selected because it centered the emotional experience mood tracking isn't a side feature, it's the core ritual that ties everything together.

UI Design Iterations

The visual system went through three main iterations. The final direction used warm, earthy tones greens and ambers to convey calm, growth, and Nigerian landscape references. Typography was friendly but structured, avoiding the clinical feel of purely sans-serif health apps.

System Design Decisions

  • Conversational tone: All copy uses everyday Nigerian English warm, direct, no clinical language
  • Emoji as emotional shorthand: Culturally natural way to express feelings without words
  • Passion map visual: At the end of discovery prompts, users see a visual "map" of their interests shareable and meaningful
  • Offline-first mood logging: Core mood tracking works without internet connection essential for variable Nigerian network conditions
  • Accessibility: High contrast ratios, large touch targets (min 48px), scalable font sizes

Project Outcome

Full Figma prototype onboarding through core flows
Approx. 20+ screens across key user journeys
Nigerian English copy, warm colour palette, relatable imagery
WCAG AA contrast met, 48px+ touch targets throughout

UX Audit & Reflection

If I were to take this project further, the next priorities would be: usability testing with actual Nigerian users (18–30 demographic), refining the Passion Discovery flow based on feedback, and exploring a community/peer support layer that respects cultural norms around privacy and mental health disclosure.

The most important design insight from this project: accessibility for a Nigerian audience includes cultural accessibility not just visual or technical. An app that makes users feel misunderstood will be deleted regardless of how beautiful it is.
